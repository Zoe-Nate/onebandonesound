    The Marching 100

  • Florida A&M University's "The Marching 100"

    • Of course, When mentioning HBCU marching band origins, you have to pay homage to a key pioneer marching band located at Florida A&M University. Although not the first marching band known, they were the first to conduct a field show in this show-style that exploded a new era of entertainment at sporting events that we see today. Similar to a theatrical performance, a field is performed with a theme and story to tell with music and movement. Florida A&M was not the first field performed, but was the first HBCU field performed in traditional or show-style. Show style or traditional bands are different from core style, core style is relative to more military-based bands that were popular in the past, and "traditional or show-style" is entertainment-value-based by playing popular radio tunes, make intricate pictures on the field, and dance simultaneously on the field.

    Who did it first?

    In the military, black regiments had their bands since the 1860s and recruited more in their community through performances. Around the Civil War, an estimated 200,000 black men were recorded enlisted in the army and after marching band became an important part of American society. Although equal education opportunities were not offered to African Americans at the time so black bands were self-taught and were gaining a following as well as professionally taught bands. There have been plenty of black bands going around traveling in their area and performing around in parades such as the culture in New Orleans, Louisiana way before they were put on black collegiate campuses. This era was known as the "Golden Age of the American Brass Band", from 1880 through 1910 started the entertainment factor of a marching band and became a part of American society. The first HBCU band was to be known as the Tuskegee Normal Brass Band at Tuskegee Institute in 1906 under the direction of Captain Elbert V. Williams. Other bands that we know today followed in popularity later in the 19th century such as FAMU, Alabama State, Kentucky State, etc.
